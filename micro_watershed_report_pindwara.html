Surface Water Area

0.0% of the micro-watershed area, equivalent to 0.53 hectares, is covered by surface water bodies. The surface water presence has remained steady during 2017-22. During the monsoon, we observed a 44.7% decrease in surface water area during drought years (2021 and 2022), as compared to non-drought years. This decline serves as a sensitivity measure, highlighting the significant impact of drought on surface water availability during the primary crop-growing season. In non-drought years, surface water in kharif typically decreases by 38.5 % in rabi. However, during drought years, this seasonal reduction is significantly higher, reaching 4.5 % from kharif to rabi. This underscores the need for enhanced water conservation measures during kharif to stabilize surface water availability and support rabi agriculture under drought conditions.

Cropping Intensity

The cropping intensity of this area has stayed steady at 1.3. It might be possible to improve cropping intensity through more strategic selection, while keeping equity in mind, of rainwater harvesting or groundwater recharge structures.The observed 4.7 % reduction in cropping intensity during drought years (2021), compared to non-drought years, reveals a marked sensitivity of agricultural productivity to water scarcity. This decline underscores the critical need for farmers to adopt drought-resilient practices, such as constructing water harvesting structures. By capturing and storing rainwater, these structures can provide a crucial buffer against drought periods, helping to stabilize cropping intensity and sustain productivity even in water-stressed conditions.

Drought Analysis

An analysis of identified drought years 2021 and 2022 reveals significant insights into the underlying rainfall patterns such as dry spells and deviations from normal precipitation. During the identified drought years, the longest dry spell recorded in 2021 lasted 5.0 weeks and 2022 lasted 10.0 weeks. Prolonged dry spells can lead to significant reduction in crop sown area or crop damage.

Water Balance

A water balance study of the micro-watershed indicates whether it is helping conserve water or deplete water from the underlying aquifer. Incoming water into a micro-watershed is through rainfall. Some of this is lost as runoff while the rest percolates into the ground. Crops pull some of this groundwater, and borewells for irrigation may pull groundwater from deeper aquifers too. A positive water balance indicates that less of the incoming water is lost, while a negative water balance indicates that the micro-watershed takes away more water. This however should not be a cause for complacency - over-extraction should be reduced, because over the years it seems that the rate of extraction of groundwater has increased. In the micro-watershed, 2017-2018, 2018-2019, 2019-2020, 2020-2021, 2021-2022, 2022-2023, 2017, 2018, 2019, and 2020 were good rainfall years, bringing an average annual rainfall of approximately 254 mm with monsoon onset between 24 June to 27 June. This favorable rainfall pattern resulted in negative groundwater recharge, with average groundwater change of 18.6 mm, indicating depletion of groundwater resources. During these years, around 21.9 % of the rainfall became surface runoff, offering potential for water harvesting, although this should be evaluated carefully so as to not impact downstream micro-watersheds. In contrast, 2021 were bad rainfall years, leading to reduced annual rainfall averaging around 440.1 mm. Limited water availability in these years resulted in negative groundwater changes, with an average depletion of 77.6 mm. Runoff in these years is reduced to 14 % of total rainfall, diminishing the harvestable water.
